A New Lid - the BMW System 6

Date Wed 24 August 2016 Tags motorcycling

So I dropped my reasonably new Shoei RF-1200, from about handlebar height, onto my driveway, which calls in to question whether the helmet is still safe to wear. Is it even 2 years old yet? Shoei offers a helmet scanning service to confirm whether it is still safe, but that would mean no riding while I send it away.

So, a dealer had a bunch of BMW System 6 lids on sale and in my size, and I picked one up for a relative song. Maybe I’ll send the Shoei off for testing now that I have another lid to wear.

The System 6 is a flip-up style helmet, manufactured for BMW by Schuberth. Mine is “Sapphire Black Metallic”, or “shiny sparkly black” in English. Here it is in some other colour.

A Short Term Review

I’ve only worn the helmet for a half dozen or so hours, which includes a day trip involving maybe 4 hours of continuous wearing time, so this is a somewhat preliminary review.

Fit & Finish

At first blush, the helmet is not as comfortable as my Shoei, but so far it is “comfortable enough”. Construction is great. A very minor issue: Opening and closing the visor has a bit of a cheap clicky plastic-y feel to it, where opening and closing the visor on the RF-1200 feels quite solid. It’s hard to match Shoei on the fit and finish fronts and the S6 is no slouch.

Fit is a very individual thing. For me, the internal shape feels somewhat shorter and wider than the RF-1200. Where the RF-1200 seems to evenly grab my noggin at all points, the S6 feels like it is gripping mainly my forhead and the back of my head, while it almost feels like there’s a gap on the left and right.

The “cheek pads” are pretty tight around the TM joint, but time may allow those to get worked in a bit, and if not there are other sizes of cheek pads available.

Features

I’ve been wanting a flip-up lid for a while and I had been eye-balling the S6, the Shoei Neotec, and the Schuberth C3 Pro. Had the sale price on the System 6 not been so attractive, I would have given the Neotec a close look, being somewhat of a “Shoei guy”.

The mechanism for lifting the chin bar on the S6 is easy to use, and there’s a decently satisfying click when you close the chin bar.

Pinlock shield is standard.

Internal sunvisor is great. Easy to operate and no more packing a clear visor with me when I expect to be out after sun-down.

I’m also liking the ratchet style chin strap. So much easier to operate than the old D-ring.

Ventilation

Seem plenty adequate. The top vent lets plenty of air in to cool your noggin. Unlike my RF-1200, when the chin vent is open, there is little to no air on your face. I think that is good? With the RF-1200 I occasionally have to close the chin vent on long rids because the air flow is drying out my eyeballs.

Another very minor niggle: The chin vent mechanism is super easy to operate. It is also super easy to close if you are manipulating the chin bar or the main visor. Many times I found I had accidentally closed the chin vent when I did not mean to.

ECE 22.05

Helmet safety standards are weird. This lid is ECE 22.05 certified. ECE 22.05 is approved for use in Ontario. Not sure about other provinces. The user manual explicitly states the helmet is not legal to wear in the US, as it is not DOT certified. I’m not expecting to get pulled over on my trip to Maine next year, but helmet standards are still weird.

Comms

I plan to get a comm system to pair with my GPS, phone, and riding partners. The BMW comm system is intriguing for its specific integration with the System 6 helmet, but the technology seems dated and the price is a bit out there.

Compare the Bluetooth 2.1 based BMW system at about $570CAD to the Bluetooth 4.0 based Sena 20S at about $370CAD. The BMW system claims a best case bike-o-bike range of 100 metres, while the newer Sena and Cardo systems claim more than 1km. Maybe the newer BT standards don’t really offer a motorcyclist much over the older standard? No idea.

I really appreciate the level of integration that the BMW system has, but BMW needs to do a better job of keeping up.
