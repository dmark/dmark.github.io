A New Ride - the BMW R1200GS

Posted on Sun 07 August 2016 in Live to Ride, Ride to Live

A Winner

I have a 2016 R1200GS in “Frozen Dark Blue Metallic” on order with a variety of gizmos including BMW’s Navigator V GPS system. Being only 172cm tall, I’m getting the bike lowered to better fit my height. I hope to have the bike within a few weeks.

I Ride

I received my motorcycle license in the Fall of 2011. I also bought my first and, until now, only motorcycle at the same time: a previously enjoyed 2006 Yamaha FZ6 sport touring bike. The previous owners didn’t do much with it, there being only 9,000kms on it at the time. Since then I’ve added about 40,000kms to the odo. I commute to work by motorcycle most days from March until well into December, do occasional joy rides on the weekends, and I love long distance motorcycle touring.

The FZ6 is a blast to ride and I love it now as much, and possibly even more, than ever. However, while it is a sport touring rig, it lacks certain creature comforts for the long road, like cruise control and power outlets. The saddle is good for 1.5 to 2 hours at a time, but my usual riding crowd are upgrading to bikes capable of over 2 hours, and I would like to do the same. Some of the missing features on the FZ6 can be added (power) but I decided I wanted to upgrade to something with modern technology and touring features as stock.

I’ve been shopping new bikes for a year or more now and recently narrowed the field of options to a single mid-size bike, the Triumph Tiger 800 XRT, and three heavyweights:

  • Yamaha FJR1300
  • Triumph Tiger Explorer
  • BMW R1200RT
  • BMW R1200GS

Triumph Tiger 800

I was able to ride a Tiger 800 demo bike for a good 5 hours and it is just fantastic. If you like that style of bike, you’d be hard pressed to find something else that offers everything the 800 XTR offers. I had been looking at Yamaha’s FJ-09 as well, but it lacks cruise control, which is a deal breaker for me. Likewise BMW’s F700 and F800 don’t have quite the same feature set.

I had actually put down a deposit on a 2017 Tiger 800 XRT. However, I knew the mid-size bike was going to be a temporary measure on my way to a heavyweight. I decided to skip the half-step and go straight to the heavy, in part because, why finance the 800 now, only to finance a heavyweight later. I had, and maybe still have, some concerns about using the larger bike for daily commuting. Nonetheless, I’ve decided to go with the heavy.

Yamaha FJR1300

My experiences with the FZ6 have made me somewhat of a “Yamaha guy”. I really want to love the FJR. It is drop-dead gorgeous, and a fantastic ride by all accounts (I haven’t ridden it). But it is old. The basic platform was introduced in 2001. Yamaha has made incremental changes to the bike over the years, including some very cool changes to the gearbox for 2016, but it still shows its age in features like the very basic ride modes. If Yamaha gave the FJR a full reboot, I would be giving it a good deal more attanetion.

The Beemers

I took advantage of BMW’s demo roadshow on multiple occasions this year to ride the K1600GT, the R1200RT (twice), and the R1200GS (twice). The K was great, but just too much power for my liking. It’s a race bike in full touring rig. Most recently I rode the RT and the GS back to back, and without any doubt I found the GS to be both more comfortable and more rideable for me.

I had some specific issues with the RT. The front end seemed to waver at low speeds (30 - 50kph), and there is a ton of vibration in handlebars at 4500 - 5000RPM which is a rev range you frequently find yourself in. The GS had neither of these problems. It’s only ‘issue’ is its height. It is super-tall in its stock setup, and I am not! I did the demo rides with the low seat option and that was manageable.

There is one very annoying issue with all the BMWs: The switch gear on the handlebars has absolutely not tactile feedback at all. In particular the turn signal switch. With gloves on you cannot feel it moving. You actually have to look at your hand to see if you are pushing the switch properly, or actively check the display to confirm the signal is on. I’ve played with the switch gear on almost every BMW bike model and they are all the same.

Triumph Tiger Explorer 1200

Unfortunately Triumph does not offer any sort of demo program for the Explorer in Canada. While I am sure the Explorer is a very good bike (generally very positive reviews all around) and despite a strong affinity for the Triumph brand, the inability to ride a demo bike was a major factor in my eventual decision to go with the BMW R1200GS. Maybe I’ll take a trip to the UK at some point and take an Explorer out, but for now my decision is made, and I am bloody excited!
